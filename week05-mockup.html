WEEK 05 OVERVIEW

Coded Exposure

How visibility itself is engineered unevenly across race, and what it costs to be over-seen or unseen.

What happens when a system is built to see some faces better than others?
1. Read2. Watch the walkthrough3. Run the audit4. Reflect
⏱ About 90 minutes  ·  read 40 / watch 20 / do 25 / reflect 5
PURPOSE

Coded exposure is Benjamin's third dimension of the New Jim Code: visibility is distributed by design. This week you learn to see that distribution and name its harm, building on the intersectionality you met in Week 2 and setting up the accountability work ahead.

LEARNING OUTCOMES

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

Define coded exposure as the uneven, designed distribution of visibility across race.
Explain the double bind of being over-exposed and under-recognized at the same time.
Describe the coded gaze and cite the Gender Shades evidence for it.
Identify a real example of coded exposure and add it to your Living Cartography.
GUIDING QUESTIONS

Hold these while you work:

+What are the different meanings of exposure, and how does Benjamin use them at once?
+Is visibility a trap? When is being seen a protection, and when a risk?
+Where have you seen the coded gaze, a system that reads some people well and others badly?
+Who is made hyper-visible by a system you use, and who is made invisible by it?
KEY CONCEPTS

Coded exposure distributes visibility by design

Some people are made hyper-visible to systems while others are made invisible.

Visibility cuts both ways

Being watched is a harm; not being recognized is also a harm. For racialized communities both happen at once.

The coded gaze decides whose face counts

Buolamwini's term for whose face a system is built to see.

Accuracy is not the same as safety

A system that sees you well can still put you at risk by watching and exposing you.
